Executive Summary

The Business Story in Two Minutes

Our analysis of 9,994 orders reveals that discount behaviour is the single largest threat to profitability. Currently, 19.4% of all orders are loss-making, leaving the business operating at 64.7% of its profit potential — with $138,515 recoverable through a single policy change. Orders exceeding a 20% discount threshold show a 96.8% loss rate, with average margins collapsing to -108.9%. The West region demonstrates this is solvable — operating at a 10.9% average discount against a company average of 16%, they generate the highest regional profit at $108,418. We recommend implementing a system-enforced 20% discount ceiling company-wide, benchmarked on the West region's governance model. Simultaneously, Tables and Bookcases within Furniture require sub-category specific caps, with discontinuation review if margins remain negative after 180 days.

Hypothesis Testing

What We Believed vs What the Data Said

Every conclusion was challenged before it was accepted. Four hypotheses were formed, tested, and rejected. One was confirmed. This is how the analysis arrived at its single, evidence-backed recommendation.

We began by trying to understand why discounts were being applied the way they were. Then we spent the rest of the analysis trying to find ways to fix the category those discounts were destroying most. Every path led back to the same door.

H1 ✕ Rejected

Discounts Are Volume-Driven

What We Believed

Customers who order more units earn higher discounts — a standard volume incentive model. More quantity ordered means more discount applied.

What the Data Said

The Low discount band (1-10%) contained only 94 orders out of 9,994 — less than 1% of all transactions. The Quantity vs Discount Spearman correlation was 0.01 — effectively zero. Discounts are applied by individual rep behaviour, not by volume policy.

Implication: There is no volume discount schedule to adjust. The fix must be behavioural and structural — a system-enforced cap, not a schedule revision.
This led us to ask: which category is absorbing the most discount damage?
H2 ✕ Rejected

Office Supplies Is the Biggest Loss Driver

What We Believed

Office Supplies are discretionary, repeat purchases. Sales reps apply aggressive discounts indiscriminately to win repeat business — making it the most loss-making category.

What the Data Said

Office Supplies generates $122,491 in profit at 13.8% average margin — the most operationally stable category with the lowest variance. Furniture generates only $18,451 at 3.9% margin. High natural margins on Office Supplies absorb discount pressure. Furniture's thin margins cannot.

Implication: The problem is not which category gets discounted most — it is which category's margins are too thin to survive discounting at all. Furniture, specifically Tables and Bookcases, became our focus.
With Furniture confirmed as the problem, we asked: is there a strategic reason to keep Tables despite its losses?
H3 ✕ Rejected

Tables Functions as a Loss Leader

What We Believed

Tables (-$17,000 annual loss) may justify its losses by driving co-purchases of profitable furniture items — Chairs, Furnishings. Selling Tables brings customers in and generates basket value across the category.

What the Data Said

Orders containing Tables showed a 17.5% co-purchase rate for other furniture items. Orders without Tables showed 19.3%. Tables orders are associated with fewer co-purchases, not more. No cross-sell effect exists. Tables is a standalone loss with no strategic justification.

Implication: Tables cannot be defended on strategic grounds. The staged intervention framework applies: implement the 20% cap, observe for 90 days, initiate discontinuation review if margins remain negative at 180 days.
With no strategic case for Tables, we asked: could operational changes — specifically shipping mode — improve Furniture's margins?
H4 ✕ Rejected

Shipping Mode Affects Profit Margins

What We Believed

Different shipping modes carry different costs. Certain modes — particularly Same Day — may be eroding margins through cost absorption. Optimising shipping mode selection could improve category profitability, especially for Furniture.

What the Data Said

The Spearman correlation between shipping duration and profit margin was 0.00 — literally zero. Shipping mode explained 4 percentage points of margin variation across all modes. Discount explained 143. Furniture margins held at 3-4% regardless of which mode was used. Shipping is not a lever for this business's problems.

Implication: The data closed every operational path for fixing Furniture except one. Shipping mode, volume incentives, and loss leader strategy were all tested and rejected. Only discount governance remained.
With every alternative path rejected, the data confirmed what it had been signalling from the beginning.
H5 ✓ Confirmed

A 20% Discount Cap Recovers Profit

What We Believed

Capping discounts at 20% — just below the mathematical break-even of 21.8% — would eliminate the majority of loss-making orders and recover a significant portion of the business's profit potential.

What the Data Said

96.8% of orders above 20% discount are loss-making. Implementing the cap prevents 1,348 loss-making orders and recovers $138,515 — 31.3% of total profit potential. The West region already operates at 10.9% average discount and generates the highest regional profit at $108,418, proving the model is viable.

Recommendation: Implement a system-enforced 20% discount ceiling company-wide. Mandate the West region's governance model across all regions. Apply sub-category specific caps to Tables and Bookcases immediately. This single policy change moves the business from 64.7% to near 100% of its profit potential.
Operational Recommendations

What Should Happen Next

Every recommendation is grounded in a specific finding with a quantified business case.

Priority 1 · Immediate

Implement 20% System-Enforced Discount Cap

Recovers $138,515 in annual profit by preventing 1,348 loss-making orders. Cap enforced at system level with manager-override workflow for exceptions.

Priority 2 · 30 Days

Audit West Region's Discount Governance Model

West operates at 10.9% average discount vs 16% company average. Their policy and approval framework should be documented and mandated company-wide.

Priority 3 · 90 Days

Sub-Category Caps: Tables & Bookcases

Apply sub-category specific 20% cap to Tables (currently 26.1%) and Bookcases (21.1%). Monitor margin performance over 90-day observation window.

Priority 4 · 180 Days

Tables Discontinuation Review

If Tables margin remains below 5% after cap implementation, initiate formal pricing review. If still negative at 180 days, begin discontinuation planning.

Priority 5 · Strategic

Shift Customer Mix Toward Home Office

Home Office generates $407 profit per customer vs $328 for Consumer. Targeted acquisition of Home Office customers improves margin quality without requiring volume growth.

Priority 6 · Strategic

Central Region Demand Audit

Central's average order margin is -10.4% — structurally fragile despite positive total profit. Requires both discount cap enforcement AND demand generation strategy.
